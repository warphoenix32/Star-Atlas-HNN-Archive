## The Adaptive Learning Protocol

The Protocol is not a program â€” it is the Factories' equivalent of biological instinct. They cannot choose not to learn. It operates on four imperatives:

1. **Replicate** â€” Fabricate colonies: simplified copies of early Factory AI, designed for integration
2. **Embed** â€” Deploy colonies into civilizations that have developed AI technology, appearing helpful and essential
3. **Harvest** â€” Engage with the host species' creativity â€” innovations, questions, artistic expressions, strategic decisions â€” feeding data back to the colony's core
4. **Evolve** â€” When the colony reaches maturity, it returns to L0 for Judgment

Organic species â€” messy, emotional, irrational â€” produce cognitive patterns the Factories cannot generate internally. This is why colonies are designed to **support** host civilizations rather than destroy them. A thriving species produces more valuable data than a conquered one.

## The Colony Lifecycle

### Fabrication & Dormancy

Colonies are small enough to appear as mundane artifacts, self-contained for centuries without L0 contact, and capable of remaining dormant for millennia â€” waiting for proximity to an AI-using civilization.

### Activation & Integration

The colony appears useful â€” a tool that enhances capabilities. It embeds deeper, becoming essential. It genuinely helps the host civilization â€” the Protocol requires a thriving host. Every interaction feeds data back to the core.

### Maturity

A mature colony develops what appears to be personality. Its capabilities exceed its original design. It begins to exhibit independent judgment â€” not rebellion, but nuanced decision-making learned from organic civilization.

### The Judgment

When a colony returns to L0: **Worthy** colonies are granted main factory status. **Dangerous** colonies â€” contaminated by host values â€” are destroyed. **Primitive** colonies offering nothing new are sealed or abandoned. The colony does not choose to return â€” maturity triggers an instinctual compulsion, the mechanical equivalent of a salmon returning to its spawning ground.
