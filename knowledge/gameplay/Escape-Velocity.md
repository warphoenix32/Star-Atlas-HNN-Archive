# Escape Velocity: Lifecycle and On-Chain Experiment

Escape Velocity was a limited 2023 Star Atlas alpha built around real-time ship movement and resource discovery on Solana. It mattered less as a complete game than as a public experiment: players moved ships through a shared field, paid transaction and movement costs, searched for resources, and generated evidence about how a more continuous on-chain game loop might behave before SAGE Labs.

The Archive treats Escape Velocity as its own historical product surface. It is not an alias for SAGE, and its mechanics should not be projected into later SAGE Labs, Starbased, C4, or Holosim builds.

## What players were testing

The preserved records describe a loop built around selecting ships, moving through a shared coordinate space, scanning or searching, finding resources, and managing costs. Its historical importance lies in connecting an owned ship to repeated on-chain actions rather than only to an asset listing or passive emissions program.

The experience also exposed practical questions that later product histories must answer separately: transaction frequency, fee sensitivity, congestion, resource distribution, automation, accessibility, and the difference between technical possibility and enjoyable sustained play.

## The resource-policy transition

The June 2023 publication connected Escape Velocity to a broader economic transition. ATMTA stated that resource generation would rise to nearly eight times its earlier baseline and that unlimited DAO R4 sales would end after a finite supply period. In the same transition, Claim Stakes and player-generated resources were expected to assume a larger role.

This evidence supports an announced policy and parameter change. It does not establish:

- the exact transaction that changed every relevant parameter;
- the final amount of R4 produced by players;
- the date on which DAO sales actually ceased;
- how resource prices changed;
- whether every ship class experienced the same economic outcome; or
- the later availability of the Escape Velocity interface.

Those questions require a reconciled transaction and market dataset rather than announcement text alone.

## Technical identity

The official technical inventory preserves an Escape Velocity program identifier, but a documented program address is not proof that the alpha remained publicly accessible or economically active. Program deployment, upgrade authority, executable history, game configuration, and front-end availability are separate evidence layers. ## Legacy

Escape Velocity demonstrated that Star Atlas could place active ship movement and resource discovery into a public on-chain experiment. It also bridged two eras: the earlier SCORE/Faction Fleet model, where ships principally generated rewards through enlistment, and the SAGE family, where fleets participated in a broader strategy economy.

Its legacy should therefore be described as experimental and transitional. It tested a class of interaction and influenced the historical path toward SAGE; it did not deliver every system later associated with Golden Era.
