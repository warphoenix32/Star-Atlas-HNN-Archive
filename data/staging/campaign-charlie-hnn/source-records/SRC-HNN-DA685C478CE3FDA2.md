Star Atlas Economic Report: The Good, The Bad, The Alpha
Posted by Cpt. August | Jul 3, 2022 | Economics
On June 30, the ATMTA, Inc. Department of Economics released its first in-depth report on the Star Atlas economy, analyzing SCORE activity and the wider Star Atlas ecosystem in detail. Interestingly, the econometricians at ATMTA chose to adopt a labor economics framework, designating staked ships as the Star Atlas labor force, with an employment rate equivalent to the amount of time spent in SCORE earning ATLAS (i.e. only when supplied with resources). Using labor economics to describe an NFT staking program may seem odd at first, but it squares nicely with the Star Atlas vision of a decentralized, virtual nation-state, with a digital workforce powered by gamers, builders, and investors. While the report makes an effort to highlight the positive metrics, the impacts of the bear market were clearly seen throughout the data. Here are my key takeaways for the Star Atlas economy, the future outlook, and some hidden alpha you might have missed.
Bad News First: Impact of the Bear Market
As SCORE players and investors are well aware, Star Atlas assets have taken a beating in the aftermath of the 2021 crypto/metaverse bubble. Long gone are the heady January days of swapping earned ATLAS for .1 USDC, and many ships trading well above VWAP. The above graphs, from page 4 of the report, confirm that the decline of ATLAS and POLIS basically tracked the broader collapse of the Metagaming asset class. In fact, ATLAS and POLIS performed slightly worse during Q2 2022. I believe this can be traced to the continued growth of ATLAS emissions in SCORE, and the limited utility for both tokens during this period.
The most troubling statistic, in my view, was the decline in active players over the last six months. According to page 16 of the report, daily active players declined from 15,710 on January 1st to 10,070 on May 31st. This can be felt in the Star Atlas Discord, which is mostly populated by the same core group of highly engaged players, and by guild leaders struggling to attract fresh recruits for their organizations. This trend must be reversed to make the Star Atlas economy sustainable. Every new player, no matter how small their initial investment, adds vitality to the game ecosystem, and their positive experiences can generate word-of-mouth buzz that brings in more players. With a referral program launching soon to incentivize this kind of growth, and other releases coming for #StarAtlasSummer, we hope to see some positive turnaround here in the next report (about three months from now).
The Good News: Active Players Continue to Reinvest
Despite major macroeconomic headwinds and no major Star Atlas releases in the first half of 2022, the report highlighted the resilience shown by the SCORE ecosystem. Despite the declining number of active players, the total number of ships employed in SCORE and refilled daily increased from 197,858 to 272,439. A significant amount of this activity was driven by players accumulating low-cost, high-yield Fimbul Airbikes. One notorious Ustur “bike whale” has amassed 45,000 of the cheapest ship in the game. The average number of ships per player has increased from 13 to 27 thanks to this continuous investment, but I do wonder what that figure looks like if you exclude the outlier whales with enormous fleets.
Thanks to the increase in player fleet size, the falling employment rate for all classes of ships was offset, resulting in higher total daily ATLAS output. Unemployment for Capital and Commander-class has also leveled off in Q2 2022 after rising in Q1, although the unemployment rate for smaller ships has increased continuously. As the conclusion of the report states, “The total number of ships has increased, and ships brought in early on have not seen the withdrawals one would expect after a significant market downturn. S.C.O.R.E. has been sticky… aggregate labor hours increased” (pg. 21). This is a good sign for the long-term viability of the game economy.
Hidden Alpha in the Footnotes
As the Hologram’s resident ship connoisseur, I was pleasantly surprised to get some clarity on a question that has been vexing me: just how much better is a Medium component versus a Small component? In footnote 4 on page 8, we get an answer: “two Small class components equate to roughly 73.5% of the output of one Medium component. The general rule of thumb is that component productivity increases by a factor of 2.7 with each class.” This is huge news. This means that the two Medium guns on a Fimbul BYOS Earp are the equivalent of 5.4 Small weapons, making it far and away the best-armed Small fighter released so far. This gives us hard numbers to consider when evaluating and comparing ships, something I have been eagerly waiting for. The Calico ATS Enforcer’s Capital-sized weapon is looking pretty attractive now, too.
Future Outlook
The conclusion of the report optimistically asserts that we have reached “a halt in the economic slowdown and the start of economic recovery” (p. 21). Is this really true? Certainly, the bleeding in ATLAS/POLIS slowed in the last month or so, and ship prices are no longer in freefall. The soon-anticipated release of POLIS staking for long lockup periods in the Star Atlas DAO, and the later release of ATLAS staking, should represent significantly bullish supply shocks for both tokens. Utility for ATLAS and ship NFTs within SCREAM could drive a positive demand shock for both assets. The Solana network is faster and better than ever with recent upgrades, and is generating buzz with groundbreaking initiatives like the Solana Mobile Stack and associated smartphone hardware.
Despite the presence of these bullish factors in the near term, the outlook for the macro economy and crypto sector remains uncertain at best. Insolvency appears to be spreading throughout centralized lenders and exchanges, with Voyager being the latest to halt user withdrawals, deposits, and trading, as well as disabling their debit card service. The collapse of many high-profile crypto products and services is eroding the trust of retail consumers in the sector as a whole, potentially harming Star Atlas’ user acquisition. Combined with historic corrections across a wide spectrum of tradfi assets, high inflation worldwide, and ongoing geopolitical instability, it is difficult to imagine a strong recovery for risk assets like metagaming tokens in the near future.
That being said, Star Atlas has yet to tap into the traditional gaming market. This has been due mainly to a lack of existing gameplay and this demographic’s hesitancy to explore NFT games (being traumatized by their experiences with predatory microtransaction models). Although these consumer wallets will be tightly squeezed by inflation and other pressures, if Star Atlas can deliver enjoyable gameplay with SCREAM and an immersive, highly social experience, the potential for hypergrowth remains strong beyond the six month time horizon. I maintain a buy-and-hold outlook on Star Atlas assets in the meantime.
