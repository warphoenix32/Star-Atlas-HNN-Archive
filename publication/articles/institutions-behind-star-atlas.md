# The Institutions Behind Star Atlas

“Star Atlas” can refer to a game, a fictional universe, a company’s product family, a community or a governed ecosystem. That flexibility is useful in ordinary conversation and dangerous in historical writing.

The public record contains several institutions acting around the same project: ATMTA, the Star Atlas DAO, the Star Atlas Foundation and the Star Atlas Council. A proposal may involve all four, yet each one speaks and acts through a different authority.

## ATMTA: developer, operator and publisher

ATMTA, Inc. is the company most closely associated with building and operating Star Atlas products. It publishes product announcements, roadmaps, technical material, economic reports, partnership announcements and other official communications.

That makes ATMTA a high-authority source for its own terminology, intentions and release statements. It does not make every ATMTA claim independently verified, and it does not turn the company into the DAO.

ATMTA may author a proposal, receive an authorization or implement a product change. Eligible POLIS holders still supply the formal vote under the captured governance mechanism. The Foundation and Council may occupy other steps in the same process.

The archive can describe ATMTA’s public operating role more confidently than its full corporate history. Complete ownership, financing, department and leadership chronologies have not yet been preserved.

## The DAO: an electorate and decision process

The Star Atlas DAO is best understood as a governance system through which people controlling locked POLIS voting power consider proposals. It is not an operating company and not every person who participates in Star Atlas.

The DAO can approve policy, elect Council members and authorize governed economic actions. Passage records the electorate’s decision. It does not execute code, sign a transaction, deliver a product or prove an outcome.

This distinction protects both the voters and the historical record. An implementation failure should not be rewritten as though the vote never occurred, and a successful vote should not be celebrated as completed work when later evidence is absent.

## The Foundation: legal and administrative capacity

The Star Atlas Foundation is the legal and administrative implementation body described in the captured governance framework.

Its supported roles include administering proposal requirements, maintaining procedural continuity and mediating implementation within legal, compliance and safety constraints. A compliant proposal can advance to voting; a passed proposal can then require real-world execution.

Administrative authority is not independent legislative sovereignty. The Foundation does not become the electorate simply because it operates part of the process. Conversely, DAO authorization does not eliminate the need for lawful administration.

Current officers, signers and delegated duties require dated records. The institutional model is clearer than the full roster of people who occupied it over time.

## The Council: elected process stewardship

The Star Atlas Council is an elected governance-process steward. Its documented functions include helping proposal authors, operating parts of the process, administering delegated programs and producing operational assessments.

The first final Council roster is supported by the PIP-7 election record. Later elections contain unresolved winner identities. Historical office should not be presented as current service without later seating or role evidence.

Council reports are also a particular class of evidence. If the Council says that a milestone is complete or a payment was made, the archive can accurately report the Council’s assessment. Independent verification still needs the transaction, deliverable or other primary artifact.

## Treasury programs are not organizations

The Ecosystem Fund is a bounded policy mechanism and wallet, not an independent legislature or the whole treasury. The DAO Treasury account is a specific account, not a synonym for every locker, emissions program or governed asset.

These distinctions may sound technical, but they determine who can authorize, administer, custody, transfer and verify funds. Entity precision is the foundation of a trustworthy later graph.

## People, offices and roles

An institution is not the same thing as the people serving it. Founder, executive, employee, Foundation officer, Council member, proposal author, program recipient and publisher are different roles.

Roles are also time-bound. A person described as holding a title in 2022 should not be presented as the current officeholder in 2026 without dated evidence. Election establishes a term only to the extent that the result and later seating record support it.

This is why the archive keeps an actor index and a role registry rather than turning every name in an announcement into a permanent biographical fact.

## How an action moves through the system

A simplified institutional sequence can look like this:

1. a person or organization authors a proposal;
2. the process administrator reviews it under the rules;
3. POLIS voting power decides the proposal;
4. the Foundation or a delegate handles lawful administration;
5. the Council may perform an assigned program or assessment role;
6. ATMTA, a recipient or another operator may execute the authorized action;
7. transactions, releases or deliverables establish what happened next.

Not every proposal uses every step, but no institution should inherit another’s authority merely because the Star Atlas brand connects them.

## What remains unknown

The repository still needs ATMTA corporate filings and ownership history, current Foundation officers and signer records, later Council seating notices, formal delegation instruments and dated role-change announcements.

As of July 23, 2026, the institutional boundaries are well supported. The complete human and legal history inside those institutions remains a research project.
