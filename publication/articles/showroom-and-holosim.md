# The Showroom and Holosim

The Unreal Engine 5 Showroom and Holosim gave the public two very different windows into Star Atlas. One emphasized a high-fidelity client, ships and environments. The other used a browser-based fleet simulation to test strategy systems and seasonal progression.

They share a universe and some adjacent concepts, but they are not two builds of one product. Their release language, access paths and evidence boundaries need separate histories.

## The Showroom: public access to an Unreal environment

In September 2022, Star Atlas published instructions for entering a pre-alpha Showroom and announced its launch through the Epic Games Store. The phrase **pre-alpha** is essential. It records genuine public access while preserving the limited maturity claim made at release.

The Showroom was a named product surface inside the wider Unreal Engine client family. “UE5” describes the technology family; it does not make every later Star Atlas Unreal build an alias of the Showroom.

Official material associated the Showroom with high-fidelity ships and environments and with expanding demonstrations or gameplay tests. Those publications establish how ATMTA positioned the build and which content it named. They do not prove feature completeness, uninterrupted access or delivery of the full MMO vision.

In June 2023, ATMTA announced Showroom R2.1 in connection with a racing season. That is evidence of a named update and content expansion. The archive does not yet preserve a complete release-note chain after R2.1.

By July 2026, the official site still linked an Unreal Engine client through the Epic Games Store. The captured page did not make the exact lineage between that current client and every historical Showroom build explicit. The archive therefore records current Unreal access without silently declaring the old Showroom surface continuously live.

## Holosim: a browser test that became a seasonal product

Holosim entered the public record in June 2025 as a free-to-play test environment. Official posts described fleet management, resources, tactical combat, routes, tutorials, quests and a SAGE-branded assistant. They also said the environment would test features before mainnet.

That “before mainnet” language defines the evidence boundary. A mechanic available in Holosim is not automatically evidence that the same mechanic deployed to a production on-chain system.

Holosim then developed through named seasonal chapters. Star Atlas announced Season 1, Chapter 1 for August 20, 2025 and stated on that date that it was live. The associated reward figure is an official program statement, not a verified payout ledger.

Chapter 2 demonstrates why planned and actual dates must remain separate. A February 2026 launch was missed because of technical problems. A later announcement rescheduled the chapter for March 10, and first-party records then stated that it was live on that date.

The Chapter 2 record described faction storylines, territory control through Dominion Points, mining achievements, NPC marketplace orders, notifications, a battle log and a revised combat interface. It also reported a migration from a relay architecture to direct RPC data. These are first-party claims about the released build, not independent technical benchmarking.

## Two different meanings of “live”

For the Showroom, “live” or “launched” referred to public access to a pre-alpha Unreal client. For Holosim, the historical record moves from public test to named seasonal chapters within a browser simulation.

Neither label means that the larger Star Atlas roadmap was complete. Product availability, feature activation, economic persistence and mainnet deployment answer different questions.

Holosim’s `zATLAS` illustrates the point. It is documented as an in-simulation currency and must not be confused with the on-chain ATLAS token. Holosim can test Fleet Command-style play without proving that a separate Fleet Command roadmap surface reached general release.

## What these products reveal

Together, the Showroom and Holosim show two recurring Star Atlas development patterns. The project has used publicly accessible environments both to demonstrate its imagined universe and to expose narrower systems for testing, iteration and community participation.

That makes release history more valuable than a simple feature list. Dates, build names and access conditions tell readers what people could actually reach, while qualifications such as pre-alpha, test, chapter and PTR prevent a limited surface from being mistaken for the entire game.

## Remaining questions

The Showroom history needs every post-R2.1 release note, build identifier, Epic Games Store version record, access change and explicit successor statement. Holosim needs versioned deployment evidence, reward-payment records, performance data and a clearer product relationship to Fleet Command, SAGE and later test environments.

As of July 23, 2026, the Showroom is strongly supported as a pre-alpha release in 2022 with an R2.1 update in 2023. Holosim is strongly supported as a public test in 2025 and a live, updated browser surface through Chapter 2 in 2026. Neither history proves completion of every adjacent roadmap.
