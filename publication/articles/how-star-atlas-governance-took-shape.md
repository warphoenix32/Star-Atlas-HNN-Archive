# How Star Atlas Governance Took Shape

Star Atlas governance did not arrive in a single constitutional moment. It developed in layers: an operating governance and locking system, a public explanation of how proposals would work, formal votes on the institutions around that process, and later amendments shaped by elections and experience.

That layered history matters because four names are often treated as though they describe the same organization. They do not. The **Star Atlas DAO** is the electorate and decision process built around locked POLIS voting power. The **Star Atlas Foundation** is the legal and administrative body described in the governance framework. The **Star Atlas Council** is an elected steward of the proposal process and selected programs. **ATMTA** is the company developing Star Atlas. Their responsibilities touch, but they are not interchangeable.

## From an operating system to a ratified framework

Official publications described an initial DAO governance and POLIS-locking module as launched in July 2022. Later that year, Star Atlas published a Sustainable Governance Framework and introduced the ATLAS Locker. These records establish that governance software and public institutional design existed before the surviving numbered-proposal votes.

In 2023, official Discord announcements documented the rollout of PIP-1 and explanatory materials. That was another milestone: the proposal system was being explained and presented to the public. The archive does not assume that every version of the 2023 text is identical to the proposal later captured on the governance portal.

The clearest formal ratification period begins in July 2024. Four proposals supplied the foundation of the captured system:

- **PIP-1** described the Star Atlas DAO, proposal process and PVP-weighted voting model.
- **PIP-2** described the Star Atlas Foundation and its administrative role.
- **PIP-3** established the Star Atlas Council.
- **PIP-4** created the original Ecosystem Fund framework.

All four passed in the preserved vote record. Passage established authorization under the applicable mechanism. It did not, by itself, prove that every account was funded, every administrative act occurred, or every promised procedure remained unchanged.

## The electorate: POLIS holders and PVP

The DAO’s formal decisions are made through voting power derived from locked POLIS. The preserved framework calls this **POLIS Voting Power**, or PVP. PVP is not a simple wallet count and is not the same thing as a transferable POLIS balance. Lock duration and controlled locked positions matter to voting weight.

This is why a governance result can never be understood from ballot count alone. The number of wallets voting YES or NO is historically useful, but the proposal mechanism determines whether weighted PVP, candidate ranking or another rule controls the result. Election PIPs require especially careful reading because an advancing candidate in a qualifying round is not yet an elected Council member.

## The Foundation: administration and implementation

Voting is only one part of governance. A proposal must be reviewed, published, administered and—if approved—implemented within legal and operational limits.

The Foundation is the body described as carrying much of that administrative and implementation responsibility. In the captured framework, it or a delegate may review whether a proposal follows the required process, seek revisions, explain rejection of a noncompliant submission and advance a compliant proposal to voting.

After passage, lawful execution remains a distinct stage. The framework allows implementation to be mediated by legal, safety and compliance considerations. That does not permit the Foundation to rewrite a vote result, and it does not make the Foundation a separate electorate. It means collective authorization still needs an institution capable of acting in the world.

## The Council: elected stewardship, not a second legislature

The Council was established to help the governance process function. Its supported roles include assisting proposal authors, maintaining governance operations, administering delegated programs and, where specifically assigned, assessing milestones or payments.

The Council is neither the DAO nor an independent legislative sovereign. Its members are elected through proposal-specific mechanisms, and its operational reports remain attributed Council assessments unless separate evidence verifies them. A Council tracker entry can be valuable evidence of what the Council reported without becoming proof that a transaction settled or a project achieved its intended outcome.

## Why the distinctions matter

The governance record becomes misleading when a chain of events is collapsed into one word such as “approved.” A rigorous history keeps at least six stages separate:

1. a proposal is drafted or announced;
2. it is published;
3. voting occurs;
4. the applicable mechanism produces a result;
5. an institution authorizes or announces implementation;
6. execution is evidenced and, where possible, independently verified.

The same distinction protects the history of failed and amended proposals. A failed amendment records a debated alternative, not a rule that entered force. A superseding proposal changes the controlling policy without erasing the earlier proposal’s historical role.

## Where the history remains incomplete

The archive still lacks a complete version history for the early governance framework, a fully reconciled copy of the 2023 PIP-1 explanatory series, and independent execution evidence for every passed proposal. Later Council election records also contain unresolved winner identities. These are limitations of the surviving record, not invitations to infer the missing steps.

As of July 23, 2026, the institutional structure is well supported: POLIS holders exercise formal weighted voting authority; the Foundation supplies legal and administrative capacity; the Council stewards the process and delegated programs; and ATMTA remains a distinct operating company. The execution history of individual decisions must still be evaluated one proposal at a time.
