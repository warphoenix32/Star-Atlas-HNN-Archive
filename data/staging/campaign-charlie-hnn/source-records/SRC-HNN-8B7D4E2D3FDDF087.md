June 28th — Since the change in Star Atlas resource production two weeks ago, Star Atlas Escape Velocity saw a rampant uptick in Bot activity. During the period, Bots were enticed to exist and thrive in the ecosystem under a lack of apparent enforcement. In a surprise announcement, the permissibility of Bots has changed overnight.
Star Atlas Takes Stance On Bot Activity
In the first part of their announcement, Star Atlas declared that they had been monitoring bot activity all along and had identified several accounts that accounted for a significant portion of Escape Velocity rewards.
According to CEO Michael Wagner, around “4% of wallets discovered 55% of the loot. We corrected that issue”. This comment, along with other comments throughout the day, indicate that ATMTA is committed to the prevention of serious exploitation of game mechanics.
In both the wind-down period of EV and in the future, we are looking to further improve on the experience for players based on the data gathered during the test. One category of insight that we are acting upon immediately is botting. We have identified a small percentage of participating wallets that have made unauthorized use of automation software (“bots”). Although there are only a handful of addresses engaged in botting, they account for a significant amount of total rewards scanned in EV to date.

Star Atlas Retroactively Punishes Botters
Continuing their announcement, Star Atlas took further actions against large scale botters in Escape Velocity by retroactively placing a cap on distributed loot and disqualifying the accounts from receiving and high tier rewards that they may have found.
In a moment of intervention, Star Atlas proclaimed that loot discovered by bots in excess of the cap over the last few weeks would be redistributed to human players proportionate to their participation in Escape Velocity.
This single action will have redistiributional wealth effects on the Star Atlas economy, arguably for the better. Since the change in R4 economic production, the prices of resources have soared with large economic stockpiles beginning to form; partially due to the enhanced rewards from Escape Velocity in conjunction with Bot activity.
With these changes in effect, the next Escape Velocity loot drop will occur on June 29th.
Star Atlas Issues Stern Warning to Bots
Furthermore, now on until its closure, any EV player whose activity is determined to be that of automated services will not receive any of the loot discovered.
Going forward, we may take more aggressive actions against wallet addresses that engage in botting activity, including by eliminating any loot scanned by a botting address. We hope that these actions and the increasing complexity of SAGE over time will slow the proliferation of botting activity. We appreciate the reports we have received on suspicious bot activity; your comments and feedback are, as always, very welcome.
The discussion over bot activity has persisted within the Star Atlas community. Time and time again, Star Atlas would refer individuals to the Star Atlas Terms of Service without providing further comment.
Now, it’s clear that Star Atlas has, and will take, a strong stance against automation that is deemed exploitative.
Community Members Push Back Against Sanctions
While the strong stance against botting was welcomed by the community, not all were in favor of the new measures. Leading the rally was Kalros who argued that the legality of bots within the ecosystem was unclear.
Kalros further argued that there should be some sort of judicial standard or transparency when punishment actions are taken, something we’ve previously reported on in the past.
Other community members were quick to point out that that Star Atlas was still a very centralized organization at this stage in development. Wagner reinforce this idea by saying that, “We are in control of key decision making right now, as it should be….but that isn’t the future we’re building towards”.
Wagner added some levity to the situation by stating that Star Atlas was able to detect bot automation with accuracy and that they were even able to detect automation that was at below human performance levels.
Another dissenter amongst the community was Bodhi, who argued that Star Atlas was taking the wrong approach towards automation and that a more proactive approach was needed.
“The only way to go is to decide what’s fair proactively, and bake rules themselves into the code in the first place.”
Star Atlas Testing the Test All Along
Officially, Escape Velocity was a test of the capacity of Solana to handle the on-chain movement programs for SAGE and to see if it could handle transactions at scale.
However, its evident that Star Atlas has been testing and collecting data outside of the publicly released purpose statement of Escape Velocity.
Comments by Senior Star Atlas Economics Director Eric Vermillion imply that Star Atlas had been temporarily permitting bot activity in order to gain data and insight on how bot-player behavior would occur.
“ I publicly said this is a test of a test- how it developed didn’t surprise me. One thing that I said we couldn’t predict was what the botting would look like exactly without running the test in the wild. The more we know the more we can anticipate how they will try to game the system we are developing. Each iteration will get more and more deep in gameplay. This action by our team was a reminder that egregious botting even in a test is against the rules we all agreed to when we signed up.”
Vermillion followed up with a comment highlighting that the strategy was to “have the opponent show their strategy and tactics early”.
For the time being, it’s clear that Star Atlas is no safe haven for bots.
