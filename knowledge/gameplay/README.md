## How lifecycle language works

The repository assigns state to the narrowest named object supported by evidence. A browser release, reward program, on-chain program, test realm, and product family may all have different states at the same time.

In reader-facing prose, the essential distinctions are:

- **announced or planned** — the publisher described an intention;
- **in development** — work was reported but public access is not established;
- **testing** — a named audience could access a limited or pre-release environment;
- **released or live** — first-party evidence says the named surface became available;
- **updated** — a later named build or revision exists;
- **superseded, deprecated, or canceled** — evidence describes replacement or discontinuation; and
- **unknown** — the archive cannot responsibly assign a narrower state.

A release does not prove every promised feature, and a current documentation page does not establish historical availability.

## Current research priorities

Fleet Command and C4 remain qualified registry entries rather than standalone histories. The archive needs dated release and build evidence, clear product lineage, and stronger mappings between products, environments, game IDs, and on-chain programs. Major products also need versioned incident, deprecation, and availability records.
