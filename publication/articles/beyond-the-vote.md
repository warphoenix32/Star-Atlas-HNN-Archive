# Beyond the Vote

A governance system is revealed as much by what fails, changes or stops as by what passes. The Star Atlas proposal record includes founding ratifications, elections, rejected amendments, superseded policies, canceled projects, terminated awards and a proposal withdrawn after passage. Each outcome answers a different historical question.

The most important rule is simple: **a successful vote is authorization, not proof of implementation**. A proposal can pass and later stall. Funding can be authorized without evidence of payment. A program can begin and later be terminated. A withdrawal after passage does not turn the earlier vote into a failure; it records a later state.

## Elections require their own logic

Election proposals cannot be forced into an ordinary YES-versus-NO template. The first Council election illustrates why.

PIP-6 was a qualifying round. It identified twelve candidates who advanced, but it did not fill the five Council seats. PIP-7 was the final election and supports the first final roster: `jp`, `funcracker`, `king_bryan`, `bodhi_tree` and `drumcarl05`.

Later election records are less complete. PIP-11, PIP-25 and PIP-27 have evidence of Council-reported passage or completed processes, yet the archive does not always preserve enough candidate-level result data to name the winners. A generic portal state cannot safely fill that gap. The correct historical result is sometimes “election occurred; winner identity unresolved.”

PIP-9 changed how backup votes were treated in single transferable vote elections, and PIP-10 modified aspects of later Council terms. These amendments show governance maintaining its own machinery through proposal-specific changes rather than replacing the entire system.

## Failure is a result, not a missing record

Several proposals failed under their captured voting mechanism. PIP-13, PIP-15, PIP-19 and PIP-26 remain failed in the reviewed registry.

Their ideas are still historically meaningful. A failed proposal can show what the community debated, who supported an alternative and where consensus did not form. But its policy does not become part of the governing framework. The archive therefore preserves the proposal’s text and result without writing as though its requested action took effect.

This is especially important when a later summary discusses the same subject. Similar language in a later policy does not retroactively pass an earlier failed proposal. Each instrument keeps its own authorship, date, ballot and legal or operational effect.

## Supersession changes policy without erasing history

The Ecosystem Fund began under PIP-4. PIP-23 later replaced PIP-4 in full and restated the fund’s allocation formula, reserve rules, application limits, payment medium and Council duties.

PIP-4 remains the historical origin of the program. PIP-23 is the controlling captured policy as of this portfolio’s evidence horizon. Supersession is therefore neither failure nor deletion: it is a documented transition between two valid historical states.

## Passage can be followed by termination or cancellation

The proposal record also contains initiatives that passed but did not remain active.

- **PIP-14**, concerning THEO integration, passed and was later reported terminated.
- **PIP-17**, concerning an ecosystem media initiative, passed and was later reported canceled or terminated.
- **PIP-31**, concerning Star Seekers 2, passed and was later withdrawn after passage and reported not implemented.

These later descriptions come largely from Council-authored operational records. They are useful and should be attributed, but they do not independently verify every payment, deliverable or causal explanation. The durable finding is the sequence: vote result first, later operational status second.

## Stale interfaces are not historical adjudicators

Governance portals can preserve stale or generic status fields. A page may still say that voting is active after the recorded voting window has ended, or may use “passed” without preserving the candidate result needed to understand an election.

The archive reconciles the full evidence packet instead of selecting the most convenient field. It considers proposal text, vote dates, mechanism, ballot and PVP totals, candidate-level data, Council assessments and later records. Where these conflict, the conflict is recorded. Where the evidence is absent, the outcome remains unresolved.

## A better vocabulary for governance history

The record is clearer when the following states remain distinct:

- **proposed** — an author requested action;
- **published** — the proposal entered the public record;
- **voted** — eligible voting power participated;
- **passed or failed** — the mechanism produced a result;
- **authorized** — a passed proposal empowered an action;
- **implemented** — the responsible institution acted;
- **paid** — a transfer is evidenced;
- **completed** — the authorized work reached a defined end state;
- **independently verified** — evidence beyond the reporting actor confirms the claim;
- **terminated, canceled or withdrawn** — a later state changed the proposal’s operational history.

No single label can safely substitute for that sequence.

## What researchers still need

The highest-priority gaps are complete candidate-level records for later Council elections, Foundation implementation notices, proposal-linked transaction evidence, milestone submissions and acceptance records. Those artifacts could move some proposals from “passed, later status reported” to a more exact execution history.

Until then, the archive treats unresolved outcomes as part of the history. That restraint is not a weakness. It is what keeps a governance ledger from becoming a story of automatic success written backward from portal labels.
