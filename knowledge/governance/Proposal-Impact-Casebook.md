# Star Atlas Governance Proposal Impact Casebook

The most consequential Star Atlas PIPs did more than pass or fail. They defined institutions, elected representatives, authorized funding, replaced policy, created programs, and revealed where a successful vote could still end in cancellation, withdrawal, or missing execution evidence.

This casebook selects proposals for institutional consequence rather than archival completeness.

## PIP-1 to PIP-3: building the constitutional framework

PIP-1 formalized the DAO framework, PIP-2 established the Foundation's legal and administrative role, and PIP-3 created the elected Council model. Their importance lies in dividing authority:

- POLIS holders exercise formal PVP-weighted voting authority;
- the Foundation carries legal and administrative implementation responsibilities;
- the Council assists authors and performs governance-process and delegated program functions.

Passage established constitutional authority. It did not independently prove that every procedure, office, or safeguard was implemented immediately. [Governance Constitutional History](Governance-Constitutional-History.md)

## PIP-6 and PIP-7: the first Council election

PIP-6 was the first-round advancement stage; its leading candidates were not final winners. PIP-7 recorded the final elected Council members. Keeping the stages separate prevents a primary-election result from being misrepresented as the seated body. [Council Election History](Council-Election-History.md)

Later election PIPs have unresolved winner identities in the captured portal state, demonstrating that an election can be recorded as held without a complete final roster.

## PIP-4 and PIP-23: the Ecosystem Fund evolves

PIP-4 established the earlier Ecosystem Fund policy. PIP-23 later refreshed and superseded it. Supersession changed the governing policy without erasing the authorizations and history that occurred under the earlier framework.

The policy created a mechanism for ecosystem support; individual passed awards still require their own authorization, payment, deliverable, and outcome evidence. [Ecosystem Fund](Ecosystem-Fund.md)

## PIP-14, PIP-17, and PIP-31: passage was not completion

These cases show why a binary "passed" label is inadequate:

- PIP-14 passed and was later Council-reported as terminated.
- PIP-17 passed and was later Council-reported as canceled.
- PIP-31 passed, was later withdrawn by the author, and was reported not implemented.

The vote results remain historically valid. The later terminal states describe what happened after authorization. [Failure and Termination Casebook](Governance-Failure-and-Termination-Casebook.md)

## Program and funding PIPs

PIPs 18, 20, 21, 22, 24, 28, 29, 30, and 32 represent different forms of community program, infrastructure, creator, lore, or event funding. A passed result means the proposal was authorized under the captured governance record. It does not prove:

- a payment transaction;
- acceptance of every milestone;
- complete delivery;
- lasting use;
- independent quality or impact.

Council tracker assessments can document operational reporting, but remain Council-authored assessments unless independent evidence is linked.

## PIP-33: a large direct treasury authorization

PIP-33 authorized reimbursement of historical ATMTA expenses. Its displayed structure contains two approximately equal tranches of USD 234,756.76, each composed of 75% USDC and 25% ATLAS-equivalent value; the second tranche is delayed and reserve-conditional. The displayed components contain preserved one-cent discrepancies. Passage established authorization. Payment remains unverified without the required on-chain dataset. [PIP-33 history](PIP-33-ATMTA-Historic-Expense-Reimbursement.md)

## Failed proposals

PIPs 13, 15, 19, and 26 remain failed. Their importance lies in documenting ideas that reached a formal vote but did not receive authorization. They may influence later debate, but cannot be treated as policy or funded work.

## Evaluating proposal impact

A complete case should record:

1. proposal text and author;
2. vote mechanism and result;
3. authorization state;
4. dependency or supersession;
5. implementation evidence;
6. payment evidence;
7. delivered output;
8. observed outcome;
9. later correction, termination, or withdrawal.
