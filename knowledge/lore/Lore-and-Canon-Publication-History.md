# Star Atlas Lore and Canon Publication History

Star Atlas lore did not arrive as one finished encyclopedia. It grew through faction introductions, world-building articles, marketplace descriptions, graphic novels, product narratives, interviews, and an increasingly structured lore repository. Names, classifications, detail, and emphasis changed as the universe expanded.

The Archive preserves those layers rather than rewriting every older source into the newest vocabulary.

## Early public world-building

The earliest official publications introduced a far-future setting shaped by human, alien, and machine civilizations, a divided galactic order, and the economic and political possibilities of the metaverse. Early faction and asset descriptions often served several purposes at once: they established lore, supported marketing, and explained the identity of products being offered.

Those texts remain evidence of what Star Atlas publicly presented at the time. They should not be silently edited when later lore adds detail or changes a preferred name.

## Factions, peoples, and the Convergence War

Later publications developed the histories of MUD, ONI, Ustur, their peoples, and the Convergence War. The war became a central organizing event because it explained the formation of ONI, the relationship among the major powers, and the creation of the Council of Peace.

The [Lore Timeline](../timeline/Lore-Timeline.md) distinguishes an in-universe date from the real-world date on which a story was published. Those are separate chronologies: a fictional event can occur centuries before a page that first disclosed it.

## CORE and narrative releases

CORE Chapter 1 was publicly available in November 2022, creating an episodic graphic-novel form for the setting. Later prelude and narrative publications continued to connect lore to active product periods such as Escape Velocity.

A narrative release can establish canon while also leaving edition and revision questions. Researchers need the original file or page, publication date, version, later corrections, and relationship to other canonical sources.

## The Lore Repository

The captured Star Atlas Lore Repository represents the most systematic taxonomy currently preserved. José Eduardo Noot is a Star Atlas team member responsible for lore; although the repository is hosted under a personal GitHub identity, the operator adjudicated it as ATMTA-affiliated and canonical for lore taxonomy and nomenclature.

The Archive preserves the repository at commit `22555f277eb1496e34c0839c8f1f382842bd1d2b`. That commit is treated as a **historical canonical-source snapshot**, not a claim that the captured taxonomy is automatically the latest current canon.

The ingestion produced preferred names, entity types, aliases, relationships, source paths, and migration mappings. It also retained historical terminology and unresolved conflicts.

## Authority and conflict

For lore, the repository applies this order:

1. the captured Lore Repository for taxonomy and preferred nomenclature within its snapshot;
2. later official Star Atlas publications for newer material not yet represented;
3. historical Archive sources for what was stated at their publication time;
4. operator adjudication for archival ambiguity;
5. derived knowledge for discovery, never for overriding upstream evidence.

This order does not mean that newer terminology erases history. If an older publication used a different faction name or classification, the source keeps its original wording while the Knowledge layer records an alias or migration.

## Material divergences

The Canon Registry records several types of divergence:

- source-tree pages and generated documentation can differ;
- white-paper, marketplace, graphic-novel, and website descriptions can emphasize different details;
- taxonomy can classify an entity differently from an older narrative;
- names and hierarchies can change without a recovered correction notice;
- real-world publication dates may be absent even when in-universe dates are precise.

The correct response is not to choose whichever version is longest. It is to preserve both, identify authority and date, and record whether the difference is expansion, renaming, reclassification, contradiction, or unresolved revision.
